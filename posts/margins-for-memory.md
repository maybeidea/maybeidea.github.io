There is a kind of writing that happens in the **margins** of other writing —
the note you leave beside a paragraph, the question mark next to an equation,
the small word *why* above a line of code. This blog, in a way, is an attempt
to take the margins seriously.

Sometimes a familiar idea like $E = mc^2$ doesn't need explanation so much as
room to breathe. The way the letters sit in a sentence, the way the reader's
eye rests on them — that is already part of the meaning.

> [!note] Small principle
> A page should feel like it was made for one reader, reading slowly, with
> enough time to disagree.

## What the margin is for

The margin is not decoration. It is where:

- the claim is **tested** against a second, quieter voice,
- the **footnote** lives (which is a claim with its hands in its pockets),
- the link to another idea — something like [[On Quiet Proofs]] — can hang
  without interrupting the sentence.

## A short equation, given room

Consider the definite integral as a statement about accumulation:

$$
\int_a^b f(x)\,dx
$$

The page is asking the reader to believe that "everything between $a$ and $b$"
can be collected into a single number. That is not a small claim, and the
typography should honor it.

## A table of temperatures

| Element     | Tone                 | Purpose                         |
| ----------- | -------------------- | ------------------------------- |
| Callout     | Low saturation       | Margin annotation               |
| Code block  | Frosted glass        | Technical fragment              |
| Wiki-link   | Soft capsule accent  | Lateral navigation              |
| Formula     | Centered, roomy      | Suspended chord in the page     |

## Why memory needs margins

Memory is not a file you retrieve; it is a **room you re-enter**. The more
blank wall in the room, the more of it comes back.[^1]
